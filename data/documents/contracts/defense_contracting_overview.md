# Defense Contracting Overview

## What is Federal Defense Contracting

Federal defense contracting is the process by which the Department of Defense (DoD) acquires goods, services, and research from private sector companies. The DoD is the largest contracting organization in the US government, awarding hundreds of billions of dollars annually across all military service branches. Contract awards are publicly tracked through USAspending.gov, the official source for federal spending data.

## Key Agencies and Their Contracting Activities

The Department of Defense includes several major contracting agencies, each with distinct missions and acquisition priorities:

The Department of the Army manages Army acquisition programs including weapons systems, logistics, research, and sustainment. Major contracting commands include Army Contracting Command-Redstone Arsenal (ACC-RSA) in Huntsville, Alabama, which obligated over $34.2 billion in FY2025, and ACC-Aberdeen Proving Ground (ACC-APG) in Maryland, which focuses on research, C5ISR, and chemical-biological defense contracts.

The Department of the Navy covers both Navy and Marine Corps procurement. Major contracting activities include Naval Information Warfare Systems Command (NAVWAR) in San Diego, which handles communications, C4ISR, and electronic warfare programs, and various Naval shipyards and aviation depots.

The Department of the Air Force includes Air Force and Space Force acquisition programs. The Air Force Research Laboratory (AFRL) at Wright-Patterson AFB in Ohio is a primary hub for aerospace research, materials science, sensors, and directed energy weapons research.

The Defense Logistics Agency (DLA) provides logistics support across all services, managing supply chains for consumable items, fuel, repair parts, food services, clothing, and medical supplies.

The Missile Defense Agency (MDA), headquartered at Redstone Arsenal in Huntsville, Alabama, manages ballistic missile defense and hypersonic defense programs worth billions annually.

## NAICS Codes in Defense

The North American Industry Classification System (NAICS) categorizes businesses by industry. Key NAICS codes in defense contracting include:

541715 covers Research and Development in Physical, Engineering, and Life Sciences. This is one of the most active defense NAICS codes, covering AI/ML, hypersonics, directed energy, advanced weapons research, and autonomous systems. Major awards under this code flow through Redstone Arsenal and Aberdeen Proving Ground.

541512 covers Computer Systems Design Services. This code encompasses cybersecurity, IT modernization, cloud computing, network engineering, and zero-trust architecture implementation. Northern Virginia and Maryland are the top regions for 541512 defense contracts.

541330 covers Engineering Services including C4ISR systems, radar, electronic warfare, systems engineering, and platform integration.

541614 covers Process, Physical Distribution, and Logistics Consulting. This includes supply chain optimization, sustainment, depot-level maintenance, and logistics management services like the Army's EAGLE program.

541714 covers Research and Development in Biotechnology including biodefense, CBRN defense, and medical countermeasures, with significant activity at Aberdeen's Chemical Biological Center.

336411 covers Aircraft Manufacturing including fighter jets, helicopters, drones, and UAVs.

## Contract Types

Federal contracts come in several pricing structures:

Firm Fixed Price (FFP) contracts require the contractor to deliver at a set price regardless of actual costs, representing the lowest risk for the government.

Cost Plus Fixed Fee (CPFF) contracts reimburse the contractor for allowable costs plus a fixed fee. These are commonly used for research and development where costs are unpredictable.

Time and Materials (T&M) contracts pay hourly rates plus materials, and are common for IT services and consulting.

Indefinite Delivery/Indefinite Quantity (IDIQ) contracts are framework agreements with individual task orders. They enable rapid ordering against pre-competed vehicles and are among the most common large defense contract types. Examples include the Army's EAGLE logistics program and numerous IT services contracts.

## The Acquisition Process

The federal acquisition process follows a structured approach: requirements are defined by the requiring activity, market research identifies potential sources, solicitation is posted on SAM.gov, proposals are evaluated against stated criteria, award is made to the best value offeror, and the contract is administered through delivery and closeout.

## Small Business Programs

The DoD has mandatory small business contracting goals: 23% of prime contracts to Small Business, 5% to Small Disadvantaged Business, 5% to Women-Owned Small Business, 3% to HUBZone businesses, and 3% to Service-Disabled Veteran-Owned businesses. The SBIR/STTR programs specifically fund small business innovation, with Phase I awards up to $323,090 and Phase II awards up to $2,153,927 as of October 2024.
